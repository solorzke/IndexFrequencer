Events[edit]

  • In Fall, Magnavox discontinues the original Odyssey video game console.[1]

Business[edit]

  • New companies: Cinematronics, Enix

Notable releases[edit]

Video game consoles[edit]

  • September, Epoch releases Japan's first home video game console, the TV Tennis Electrotennis. Its most unusual feature is that the console (including the controller) is wireless, functioning through a UHF antenna.[2]
  • December, Atari and Tele-Games (a division of Sears, Roebuck and Company) release the first official home version of Pong through Sears department stores.[3]
  • Magnavox releases two new models of their Odyssey console: the Odyssey 100 and the Odyssey 200.[1]

Games[edit]

  • February, Midway releases Taito's 1974 arcade racing video game Speed Race, designed by Tomohiro Nishikado, in North America as Wheels[4] and Racer.[5]
  • February, Horror Games, founded by Nolan Bushnell, publishes its only game, Shark Jaws, intended to cash-in on the popularity of Steven Spielberg's film Jaws.[6]
  • Taito releases Western Gun, the first video game to depict human-to-human combat.[7][8] Designed by Tomohiro Nishikado, the game had two distinct joystick controls per player, with one eight-way joystick for moving the computerized cowboy around on the screen and the other for changing the shooting direction.[9][10]
  • November, Midway releases Gun Fight, an adaptation of Taito's Western Gun and the first microprocessor-based video game.[11] Taito's Western Gun used TTL-based hardware, which Dave Nutting Associates ported to the Intel 8080 microprocessor for its North American release.[12]
  • Don Daglow develops Dungeon, an early role-playing video game, for the PDP-10.[13]
  • William Crowther develops Adventure (also known as Colossal Cave and ADVENT), the first interactive fiction game, for the PDP-10.[14]
  • Rusty Rutherford develops pedit5, the first dungeon crawl game, for the PLATO system.[15]
  • dnd, the first video game to include a boss, and arguably the first computer role-playing game, wrapped up initial development. Some sources list the game as 1974; it is unclear exactly when it became playable.
  • Nürburgring 1, the first first-person racing game, was developed in Germany by Dr. Reiner Foerst.[16][17]
